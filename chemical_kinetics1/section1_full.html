1.1 What “rate” really means

Def.1.1.1 (Reaction rate, r): speed of conversion per overall reaction, normalized by stoichiometry.

Eq.1.1.1 \[ r = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{d[D]}{dt} \] Units: M s^{-1}.

Def.1.1.2 average rate over \([t_1,t_2]\): Eq.1.1.2 \[ -\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t} \]

Def.1.1.3 instantaneous rate: Eq.1.1.3 \[ r(t) = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{d[A]}{dt} \]

Def.1.1.4 initial rate: Eq.1.1.4 \( r_0 = r(t\to 0) \).

Sign sanity: reactants decrease \((d[A]/dt<0)\), products increase. Stoichiometric divisors map each species’ slope to the shared overall rate \(r\).

1.2 Differential rate laws, orders, and units

Def.1.2.1 differential rate law: Eq.1.2.1 \[ r = k\,[A]^m [B]^n \cdots, \qquad n_{\text{tot}}=m+n+\cdots \]

Def.1.2.2 units of \(k\): Eq.1.2.2 \[ [k]=\mathrm{M}^{\,1-n_{\text{tot}}}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1} \] 0th: M s^{-1}; 1st: s^{-1}; 2nd: M^{-1} s^{-1}.

1.3 Getting orders from data

Isolation / pseudo-first-order (PFO)

Eq.1.2.3 \( r=k'[A]^m,\; k'=k[B]_0^{\,n}\cdots \). Keep excess species ≥10× constant during the run; then use first‑order tools on A.

Method of initial rates

Eq.1.2.4 \[ \frac{r_{0,2}}{r_{0,1}}=\Big(\frac{[A]_{0,2}}{[A]_{0,1}}\Big)^m\Big(\frac{[B]_{0,2}}{[B]_{0,1}}\Big)^n\cdots \] Log‑regression trick: \(\ln r_0=\ln k+m\ln[A]_0+n\ln[B]_0+\cdots\).

1.4 Activities vs concentrations

Def.1.3.1 Activity \(a_i\) (ideal limits): solutions \(a_i\approx [i]/1\,\mathrm{M}\), gases \(a_i\approx p_i/1\,\mathrm{bar}\) (non‑idealities in \(\gamma_i,\phi_i\)).

Eq.1.3.1 True form: \[ r = k\prod_i a_i^{\alpha_i} \] Orders \(\alpha_i\) are empirical and need not match stoichiometric exponents.

1.5 Extent of reaction and species formation rates

Using stoichiometric numbers \(\nu_i\) (reactants negative, products positive) and the extent \(\xi\):

Eq.1.4.1 \[ r=\frac{1}{V}\frac{d\xi}{dt} \quad [\mathrm{M\,s^{-1}}] \]

Eq.1.4.2 \[ R_i\equiv \frac{1}{V}\frac{dn_i}{dt}=\nu_i\,r \] At constant \(V\), \(R_i=d[i]/dt\) and Eq.1.1.1 drops out from \(\nu_A=-a\).

Eq.1.4.3 Variable volume: \[ \frac{d[i]}{dt}=\nu_i r-\frac{[i]}{V}\frac{dV}{dt} \] Use for evaporation, gas evolution, or inflow/outflow.

1.6 Orders vs molecularity

  • Molecularity is for elementary steps; its exponents equal the step’s rate‑law powers.
  • Overall order is empirical from Eq.1.2.1; can be fractional or negative; can shift with conditions.
  • Negative order implies inhibition; zero order often implies saturation or a fixed‑rate bottleneck.

1.7 Rate normalizations

  • Volumetric rate \(r\): M s^{-1} (this doc’s default).
  • Surface‑normalized: mol m^{-2} s^{-1} (heterogeneous catalysis).
  • Mass‑normalized: mol kg_{cat}^{-1} s^{-1} (report catalyst mass unambiguously).

1.8 Measurement guardrails (making \(r_0\) real)

  • Choose an early window with conversion < 10% and signal above noise.
  • Linear fit that window; report slope, intercept, \(R^2\), and residuals.
  • Hold T constant; see Section 3 for \(k(T)\) behavior.

1.9 Mini‑practice (no numbers)

  1. Given three initial‑rate runs where only \([A]_0\) changes, extract \(m\) by ratios and by log‑regression. Do they agree?
  2. Sketch how variable volume (Eq.1.4.3) would bias an apparent first‑order plot if you ignore \(dV/dt\).
