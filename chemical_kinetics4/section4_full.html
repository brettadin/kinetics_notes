4.0 Mechanism essentials

  • Def.4.0.1 Elementary step: a single molecular event with a rate law equal to its molecularity.
  • Def.4.0.2 Intermediate: produced in one step, consumed later, absent from the net reaction.
  • Def.4.0.3 Rate-determining step (RDS): the slowest step controls the net rate in the limiting case where slowness is decisive.
  • Def.4.0.4 Validity checks: steps sum to the overall reaction; predicted rate law matches experiment; intermediates/orders are physically sensible.

4.1 Consecutive first-order A → I → P

Scheme: \(A \xrightarrow{k_1} I \xrightarrow{k_2} P\), \( [A]_0=A_0\), \( [I]_0=[P]_0=0\).

Eq.4.1.1 Species time courses

\[ [A]=A_0 e^{-k_1 t} \] \[ [I]=\frac{k_1 A_0}{k_2-k_1}\Big(e^{-k_1 t}-e^{-k_2 t}\Big)\quad (k_1\ne k_2) \] \[ [P]=A_0\Big[1-\frac{k_2 e^{-k_1 t}-k_1 e^{-k_2 t}}{k_2-k_1}\Big] \]

Eq.4.1.2 Degenerate \(k_1=k_2\equiv k\): \( [I]=kA_0 t\,e^{-kt}\), \( [P]=A_0\big(1-e^{-kt}(1+kt)\big)\).

Eq.4.1.3 Intermediate maximum: \( t_{\max}=\frac{1}{k_2-k_1}\ln\!\Big(\frac{k_2}{k_1}\Big)\). Closed form for \( [I]_{\max}\) exists but \(t_{\max}\) is what exams want.

Limiting cases: \(k_1\ll k_2\Rightarrow I\) tiny, \(r\approx k_1[A]\). \(k_2\ll k_1\Rightarrow I\) accumulates, early \(r\approx k_2[I]\).

Flux form: overall product formation rate \(r=d[P]/dt=k_2[I]\). Substitute Eq.4.1.1 to get \(r(t)\).

4.2 Approximations to eliminate intermediates

4.2.1 Pre-equilibrium approximation (PEA)

Fast reversible pre-step, slow consumption: \(A+B \rightleftharpoons I\) (fast), \(I\xrightarrow{k_2} P\) (slow) with \(k_{-1}\gg k_2\).

Equilibrium for the fast step: \(K=\dfrac{[I]}{[A][B]}=\dfrac{k_1}{k_{-1}}\). Net rate via slow step: \(r=k_2[I]=k_2K[A][B]\).

Eq.4.2.1 PEA template: \( r = k_{\mathrm{slow}}\,K_{\mathrm{fast}}\,[\text{reactants}]^{\text{stoich}} \).

Use PEA when back-reaction of the fast step dominates other fates of \(I\).

4.2.2 Steady-state approximation (SSA)

Formation and consumption of \(I\) balance quickly (not necessarily at equilibrium). Example: \(A\xrightarrow{k_1} I\), \(I\xrightarrow{k_2} P\), \(I\xrightarrow{k_3}\text{side}\).

Steady state: \(d[I]/dt\approx 0 \Rightarrow [I]\approx k_1[A]/(k_2+k_3)\). Sub into \(r=k_{\text{use}}[I]\).

Eq.4.2.2 SSA template: \( r \approx \dfrac{k_{\text{form}}\,k_{\text{use}}}{\sum k_{\text{out}}}\,[\text{reactants}] \).

4.2.3 PEA vs SSA in one glance

  • PEA: true or quasi-equilibrium preceding a slow step; typically \(k_{-1}\gg k_2\).
  • SSA: \(I\) is short-lived; typically \([I]\ll[\text{bulk}]\) and \(|d[I]/dt|\ll\) each flux term.
  • In the limit \(k_{-1}\gg k_2\), SSA reduces to PEA for this two-step scheme.

4.3 Competing pathways and selectivity

Parallel first-order decay: \(A\xrightarrow{k_B} B\), \(A\xrightarrow{k_C} C\).

Eq.4.3.1 \( [A]=A_0 e^{-(k_B+k_C)t}\), \(Y_B=\dfrac{k_B}{k_B+k_C}\), \(n_B/n_C=k_B/k_C\).

With reversibility or coverage, naive branching fails; apparent first-order constants need correction factors (e.g., \(k_{\text{app}}=k_{\text{forward}}(1-\theta)\)).

4.4 Mixed orders from mechanisms (saturation form)

Non-enzymatic saturation archetype: \(A+S \rightleftharpoons I\) (fast), \(I\xrightarrow{k_2} P+S\) (slow), total sites \(S_T\).

Eq.4.4.1 Result via PEA/SSA: \[ r=\frac{k_2\,K\,S_T\,[A]}{1+K[A]} \] Low \([A]\): first order; high \([A]\): zero order (saturation). This is the algebra that leads to Michaelis–Menten.

4.5 Diagnosing mechanisms from data

  • Intermediate hump and product sigmoid with a single exponential tail on \(A\): think A→I→P.
  • Order shifts with concentration: suspect denominators (coverage, adsorption, site saturation).
  • Arrhenius curvature across a wide \(T\): mechanism/dominant step changes. Fit segments; don’t average \(E_a\) across regimes.
  • Energy diagrams: number of maxima equals number of transition states; minima in between equal intermediates.

4.6 Worked-example blueprints

  1. A→I→P identification and \(t_{\max}\): fit \(A\) to a single exponential → \(k_1\); use early-time slope of \(P\) or hump of \(I\) to estimate \(k_2\); compute \(t_{\max}\) and compare.
  2. PEA derivation for \(A+B \rightleftharpoons I\) (fast), \(I\to P\) (slow): set equilibrium, substitute, compare with observed law.
  3. SSA with a draining intermediate: balance formation and all exits; solve \([I]\); write \(r\); check limits and units.

4.7 Micro-review

  • Consecutive first order → clean analytic solution with a single hump in \(I\).
  • PEA: fast reversible step in quasi-equilibrium; slow consumption sets rate.
  • SSA: low steady \(I\) from balanced formation and drain; no equilibrium needed.
  • Saturation and mixed orders arise from denominators like \(1+K[\text{stuff}]\).
